## What One House Is (and Isn't)

One House is a coordination layer. We help people and organizations stand up self-sustaining crisis housing operations by connecting them with partners, a plan, and tools. We do not run houses, manage intake, house participants, or deliver care.

The name comes from Board Chairman Bo Matthews: "Fix the community one house at a time." Every new operation is a single house. The network is a collection of distributed crisis houses.

**Core principles:**

- **Coordination over duplication.** We are the architect of a network, not a service provider.
- **One house at a time.** The network grows house by house.
- **Pick a lane.** Every house selects one housing type and one population to serve. The biggest mistake in this space is trying to be all things to all people.
- **Self-sustaining over perpetually funded.** Houses are designed to cover their own costs once operational. Donations seed new houses — they don't prop up old ones.
- **Systemic framing.** The problem is fragmentation and lack of structure, not bad people.
- **The operator is the primary caregiver.** Service providers extend the operator's reach. They don't replace it.

## The Problem

When someone loses their home — from job loss, a medical emergency, domestic violence, or addiction — they have very few options. Shelters are full. Waitlists are long. The organizations trying to help are overwhelmed, underfunded, and disconnected from each other.

### Key Statistics
- **621** people experiencing homelessness on a single night in North Alabama (HUD 2024 PIT Count, AL-503 CoC)
- **245** of those were unsheltered
- **$31,065** average annual cost per chronically homeless individual in public services (Central Florida Commission on Homelessness)
- **61%** reduction in ER visits after permanent housing placement (California HealthCare Foundation)
- Local churches spend tens of thousands annually on hotel stays for families in crisis because there is nowhere else to send them

### Root Cause
The problem is not a lack of people who want to help. It is a lack of coordination, structure, and a plan for how to turn good intentions into functioning programs.

## The Housing Continuum

Crisis housing is temporary, structured shelter for people in an emergency — a bridge to stability, not a permanent home.

The continuum has four active stages plus graduation. Each stage serves a different point in recovery. An operator selects ONE stage and ONE population to serve.

### Population Selection
When setting up a new house, the sponsor and operator choose a demographic to serve. Examples: veterans, elderly, families, women fleeing abuse, people in substance abuse recovery, people with mental health conditions. This focus makes houses more effective by allowing operators to develop deep expertise rather than spreading thin.

### Stage 1: Emergency Shelter
**Duration:** 1–7 days
**Purpose:** Immediate safety and basic needs (food, water, warmth).
**Setting:** Dormitory or hostel-style housing with maximum beds and shared restrooms. Security system or 24/7 on-site staff.
**Support level:** Case manager visits to perform intake assessments and connect the person to next-stage options.
**Participant role:** Applies to the program voluntarily. Cooperates with intake assessment.
**Exit criteria:** Intake assessment is complete, care plan is drafted, and a Stage 2 placement is identified and accepted.

### Stage 2: Recovery Center
**Duration:** 30–180 days
**Purpose:** Clinical stabilization — addressing the acute issues that caused the crisis (addiction, untreated mental health conditions, medical needs).
**Setting:** Co-housing or shared rooms with high bed count.
**Support level:** Assigned case manager with weekly check-ins. Regular access to clinical services (counseling, healthcare, behavioral health).
**Participant role:** Follows care plan. Engages with clinical services. Begins building daily structure.
**Exit criteria:** Clinical providers confirm the participant is stable enough to manage without frequent intervention. Participant demonstrates consistent engagement with their care plan for 30+ days.

### Stage 3: Bridge Housing
**Duration:** 6–18 months
**Purpose:** Building life skills and financial footing — employment, budgeting, community connection.
**Setting:** Private studio apartment or affordable multi-family unit.
**Support level:** Case manager with monthly check-ins. Clinical intervention only for ongoing or permanent conditions. Focus shifts from clinical care to life-skills coaching and mentoring.
**Participant role:** Covers the majority of program dues through personal income or government assistance (HUD-VASH, VA SSVF, Section 8, etc.). Takes responsibility for day-to-day living.
**Exit criteria:** Participant is covering full program dues independently. Stable employment or income source for 90+ days. Case manager confirms readiness to manage daily life without regular coaching.

### Stage 4: Launch Pad
**Duration:** 1–2 years
**Purpose:** Practicing full autonomy with a safety net. Support shifts from direct oversight to indirect availability.
**Setting:** Full apartment or multi-family unit.
**Support level:** Case manager and service providers move to an on-call role — available if needed, but not scheduling regular visits. Participant may begin mentoring others or volunteering.
**Participant role:** Lives independently. Manages all responsibilities. Builds coping skills to handle future crises without re-entering the system.
**Exit criteria:** 6+ months with no case manager contact initiated by the participant. Demonstrated ability to navigate a setback (job change, health issue, financial surprise) without crisis-level intervention.

### Graduation: Sustainable Living
This is not a program stage — it is the outcome. Graduates live in market-rate or affordable housing as part of the general population. They retain access to an alumni network for peer support and may contribute back as mentors. Graduates interested in homeownership are referred to the Affordable Housing program.

## The Four Partner Roles

Every crisis house needs four things: someone to fund it, someone to run it, a place to put it, and a network of specialists to handle what the operator can't. Most people who want to help only have one or two of these. Our job is to find the rest.

### 1. Sponsors — "The fuel"
Fund the house and set its direction.

- **Funding:** Secure donations, grants, or redirect existing funds to cover startup and operations.
- **Direction:** Choose the housing stage and the population to serve.
- **Community access:** Refer applicants and spread awareness as a trusted community touchpoint.

*Example: A church spending $5k/month on hotel stays could instead sponsor 4 recovery beds through a trained operator.*

### 2. Operators — "The engine"
Run the house day-to-day. The operator is the primary caregiver.

- **Intake:** Assess applicants and determine eligibility.
- **Case management:** Develop care plans and track participant progress.
- **Operations:** Manage finances — program dues, facility costs, staffing — to keep the house running and self-sustaining.

*Example: A 501(c)(3) that specializes in transitional living for women and children.*

### 3. Facilities — "The foundation"
Provide the physical space.

- **Ownership:** Own and maintain the property.
- **Lease access:** Offer terms that make crisis housing viable for a nonprofit operator.

*Example: A landlord leasing a 4-unit property for bridge housing, or a property owner converting a building for shelter use.*

### 4. Service Providers — "The safety net"
Specialists who extend the operator's reach so the operator stays focused.

- **Clinical health:** Licensed specialists for physical, mental, or behavioral health needs outside the operator's expertise.
- **Education and workforce:** Job training, career counseling, and financial literacy programs.
- **Peer and community support:** Mentors, support groups, and organizations that specialize in specific situations.

*Example: A shelter takes in a mother whose child has a rare disease. Instead of building new expertise, the operator connects with a nonprofit specializing in that area. The operator stays focused. The specialist gets another person to serve.*

## What One House Provides

### Matchmaking
Connect sponsors with operators, facilities, and service providers so each can focus on what they do best.

### The Playbook
Agreements between partners, operating policies, financial models, staffing guidance, and program structure. The plan that turns good intentions into a functioning house.

**Two key agreements:**
- **Program agreement** between operator and participant — defines the rules, expectations, and access to housing and services.
- **Lease agreement** between operator and facility owner — covers terms of the tenancy.

### Coaching
Help partners pick a lane and choose a role. A sponsor can fund an operator, or learn to become one. We prevent the trap of trying to do everything.

### Software
Tools for operators to manage intake, track participants through the full continuum, and report outcomes. The software captures a participant's complete journey across stages — including transitions between houses run by different operators.

### Startup Grants
One House can award grants to 501(c)(3) operators to fund expansion or prevent collapse during critical periods.

## How a House Gets Built

**Step 1: Raise your hand.** A church, nonprofit, business owner, or community member comes to One House with a desire to help. They may have funding, a building, operational experience, or simply the will.

**Step 2: Pick a lane.** Choose one housing stage and one population. Decide whether to sponsor (fund) or operate (run) — or both.

**Step 3: Assemble the team.** We match whatever is missing. A sponsor gets connected to an operator. An operator gets connected to a facility. Everyone gets connected to service providers.

**Step 4: Stand it up.** We deliver the playbook: partner agreements, financial model, staffing guidance, and startup grant if needed.

**Step 5: Open doors.** The operator runs the house. Service providers handle specialized needs. The sponsor sees their impact. One House provides software and stays connected — then starts looking for the next person ready to raise their hand.

## The Financial Model

Every house is designed to cover its own costs once operational.

**Revenue sources:**
- **Program dues** — Contributions from participants (not rent — participants are not tenants). Many participants fund this through government programs (HUD-VASH, VA SSVF, Section 8).
- **Voucher reimbursements** — Payments from government-sponsored programs that cover or subsidize participant housing costs.
- **Sponsor subsidy** — May be significant during ramp-up but should be minimal once the house reaches full occupancy and steady operations.
- **One House grants** — Awarded to fund expansion or prevent collapse, not to cover routine operations.

This changes the math for donors: a contribution to One House does not maintain an existing program. It seeds a new one. Each house that reaches self-sustainability creates permanent capacity in the network.

---

## Transition Between Stages

The operator initiates a transition based on mutual understanding with the participant that they are ready for the next stage, guided by the exit criteria above. Transitions are tracked in One House software, which records each participant's full journey through the continuum — including handoffs between houses run by different operators.

## Current Status

The Crisis Intervention program is pre-operational with a proof of concept running. A local operator is already running this model informally in partnership with our team, housing approximately 20 people in affordable housing units. No formal agreements or software are in place yet. The model is being built in public as the network takes shape.
